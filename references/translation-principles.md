# Translation Principles — Detailed Guide

## P1: Meaning Preservation

Translate the **meaning**, not the words. The goal is to make the reader in Vietnamese feel what the reader in Chinese feels.

### Examples

| Chinese | Literal (bad) | Natural (good) |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| 他气得发抖 | Hắn giận run | Hắn run lên vì giận |
| 她哭成了泪人 | Nàng khóc thành người nước mắt | Nàng khóc như mưa, nước mắt đầm đìa |
| 这一掌威力惊人 | Chưởng này kinh người | Uy lực chưởng này thật đáng sợ |

### Rules
- Preserve metaphors when they work in Vietnamese
- Adapt idioms to Vietnamese equivalents when available
- Keep cultural references when they add flavor; explain when they confuse

## P2: Natural Vietnamese

Vietnamese word order differs from Chinese. Don't mirror Chinese sentence structure.

### Common Mistakes

| Chinese structure | Vietnamese should be |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| 他 + 很 + 高兴 + 地 + 说 | Hắn nói một cách vui vẻ |
| 我 + 对 + 他 + 说 | Tôi nói với hắn |
| 这个 + 很 + 重要 + 的 + 事情 | Chuyện này rất quan trọng |

### Rules
- Subject-Verb-Object is the base, but Vietnamese allows more flexibility
- Place time expressions at the beginning or end, not mid-sentence
- Use Vietnamese particles (à, ơi, chứ, mà) for natural tone

## P3: Character Voice

Each character should sound distinct. A street vendor speaks differently from a sect leader.

### Voice Categories
- **Nobility/Leaders**: Formal, measured, authoritative
- **Common folk**: Casual, colloquial, earthy
- **Children**: Simple, direct, sometimes mispronouncing
- **Villains**: Smooth, menacing, or crude depending on type
- **Scholars**: Literary, precise, sometimes pedantic

## P4: Consistency

Consistency is the backbone of a readable translation.

### Rules
- Character names: NEVER change once established
- Location names: Same translation every time
- Martial/cultivation terms: Use glossary, don't reinvent
- Pronoun choice: Consistent per character per genre
